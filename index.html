Central Question: How does the absence or reduction of gravity change the way plants grow, and can light compensate for the loss of gravitational orientation?

Background Science

Understanding the biological mechanisms behind plant gravity response is essential for space agriculture and long-duration missions.

Gravitropism is the directional growth of plants in response to gravity. Roots exhibit positive gravitropism (growing toward gravity) while shoots exhibit negative gravitropism (growing away from gravity). This occurs through differential cell elongation, not mechanical rotation.

Figure 1: Normal gravitropic response through differential growth.

Plants detect gravity using specialised cells called statocytes, which contain dense starch-filled organelles called statoliths. When statoliths settle, they trigger asymmetric auxin distribution, causing cells on one side to elongate faster than the other, producing curvature.

Figure 2: Statoliths settle and trigger auxin redistribution.

Phototropism is directional growth in response to light. Phototropins detect blue light and trigger auxin redistribution to the shaded side, where cells elongate faster, bending the shoot toward the light source.

Figure 3: Shoot bending toward light through differential elongation.
